How the Texas Medical Board receives and investigates complaints

The Texas Medical Board (TMB) is the state agency responsible for licensing and regulating physicians and certain other healthcare providers in Texas. It receives complaints from patients, family members, employers, other healthcare providers, malpractice insurance carriers, and even anonymously. The board is required to investigate all complaints that allege a violation of the Medical Practice Act or board rules.

Unlike a civil lawsuit, a TMB investigation does not require the complainant to meet a legal burden before the board acts. The investigation begins the moment the complaint is accepted — which means the board is gathering information about you before you have any formal opportunity to respond.

The TMB complaint process — stage by stage

1
Complaint Filed & Accepted

Anyone can file a complaint against a licensed physician. The TMB screens complaints and accepts those that allege a potential violation. You may not be notified immediately — the board can begin gathering information before formal notice is sent.

2
Notice of Complaint Sent to Physician

You receive formal written notice that a complaint has been filed. This notice will typically include a summary of the allegation and a request for your written response within a specified deadline. This is the most critical moment in the entire process. Your written response here sets the record.

3
Investigation

TMB staff investigators gather information — requesting and reviewing medical records, interviewing witnesses, consulting with expert reviewers. The investigation can take 6–12 months or longer. During this period, the board is building the record that will determine whether the case proceeds.

4
Informal Settlement Conference (ISC) or Dismissal

After investigation, the board can dismiss the complaint, offer an Informal Settlement Conference (a pre-hearing negotiation), or refer the case to SOAH (State Office of Administrative Hearings) for a formal hearing. ISCs are not trials, but the outcomes — including reprimands, probation, or license restrictions — are binding if agreed to.

5
Formal Administrative Hearing (SOAH)

If the matter is not resolved at the ISC stage, the case proceeds to a formal contested case hearing before the State Office of Administrative Hearings. This is a full administrative trial — with witnesses, expert testimony, cross-examination, and a presiding administrative law judge. Resolution at this stage averages 12–24+ months from referral.

6
Board Action & NPDB Reporting

If the board takes disciplinary action — including reprimands, restrictions, probation, or revocation — the action is reported to the National Practitioner Data Bank (NPDB), which is permanent and accessible to every credentialing entity, hospital, and insurance panel in the country.

The most common mistakes physicians make

✗
Responding to the complaint without an attorney

Physicians often believe that if they explain the situation clearly, the board will understand. The board is not an advocate — it is an investigator. Unrepresented responses frequently include admissions or framing that the board uses to build the case forward.

✗
Waiting weeks before retaining counsel

The response deadline is fixed. Every day spent researching attorneys, waiting for callbacks, or second-guessing the decision is a day the board builds its record unopposed. Urgency is not panic — it is strategy.

✗
Hiring an attorney who is not a license defense specialist

General practice attorneys and civil litigators who occasionally handle board matters are not the same as attorneys who specialize in medical license defense. The TMB process, the SOAH hearing rules, and the NPDB implications require specialized knowledge.

✗
Treating the initial response as a formality

Many attorneys file a brief written response acknowledging the complaint and requesting time to gather records. This is a passive response that tells the board the case is uncontested. An evidence-anchored counter-response forces a dismissal decision.

✗
Agreeing to an Informal Settlement Conference without considering dismissal first

ISC outcomes are binding. Physicians who accept ISC resolutions often do so without understanding that dismissal was still achievable — and that the ISC outcome will be reported to the NPDB. Once you sign, you cannot undo it.
