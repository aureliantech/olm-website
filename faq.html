Medical Malpractice

This is the central question in every malpractice case. A bad outcome — even a devastating one — is not automatically malpractice. The legal standard requires proving that the provider deviated from the accepted standard of care, and that the deviation caused your harm.

The strategy session is specifically designed to answer this question for your situation. We review what happened, apply the legal standard, and tell you directly whether there is a viable claim. See how we approach medical malpractice →

In Texas, the statute of limitations for medical malpractice is two years from the date the negligence occurred, or from the date it was discovered (or reasonably should have been discovered). There is also an absolute ten-year cap regardless of discovery.

For minors, different rules apply. If you are unsure where you stand on the timeline, do not wait — call now. Missing the deadline permanently bars your claim regardless of how strong it is.

Yes. Hospitals can be held liable for the negligence of their employees, for negligent credentialing, and for systemic failures in patient care. However, physicians are often independent contractors, which can limit hospital liability depending on the circumstances.

Determining who bears liability — the physician, the hospital, or both — is part of the pre-litigation file review we conduct before filing any claim.

Yes — Texas law requires an expert report from a qualified physician within 120 days of filing suit. The report must identify the standard of care, explain how it was breached, and connect the breach to the harm. Failure to file a compliant expert report results in mandatory dismissal.

This is why we conduct a pre-litigation file review and retain qualified experts before filing — not after. Cases that cannot meet this requirement should not be filed.

Medical malpractice litigation is expensive. Expert witnesses, medical record review, depositions, and trial preparation can involve significant costs. This is precisely why we screen cases rigorously before filing — to ensure the likely recovery justifies the investment.

We discuss costs and fee structure transparently during the strategy session. You will not be surprised.

Defamation & Libel

It depends on what the review says. A negative opinion ("I didn't like the service") is generally protected speech. A false statement of fact ("This doctor committed fraud") that is published to others and causes real harm may be actionable defamation.

The distinction between opinion and false fact is often the central issue in online defamation cases — and it requires legal analysis of the specific statement and context. See our defamation practice →

Yes. Texas defamation law applies equally to statements made on social media, in posts, in comments, in direct messages that were forwarded, and in online reviews. The platform does not provide immunity to the person who made the false statement.

Social media defamation cases also have a time-sensitive evidence component — posts get deleted. If you believe you have been defamed online, contact us before the evidence disappears. Learn about our defamation approach →

Pure opinion is generally not actionable. But the line between opinion and false fact is not always clear. Courts look at whether the statement implies an underlying false fact — "In my opinion, she stole from clients" implies a factual allegation and may be actionable even though it begins with "in my opinion."

This analysis is case-specific and requires review of the exact statement and the context in which it was made. See defamation defense →

Texas allows recovery for actual damages (lost income, lost business, reputational harm), mental anguish, and — where the conduct was malicious or grossly negligent — exemplary (punitive) damages. There is no fixed cap on defamation damages in Texas for private individuals.

The value of a defamation claim depends heavily on the evidence of actual harm caused by the false statement. This is assessed during the strategy session.

Not necessarily. This firm is licensed in both Texas and Arizona, and can handle defamation matters in both states without local co-counsel. Where the statement originated is less important than where you are located and where the harm occurred.

Probate & Estate

An uncontested, straightforward Texas probate can be completed in as little as a few months. Complex or contested estates — involving disputed wills, missing assets, executor misconduct, or multiple beneficiary disputes — can take years without aggressive representation.

The single biggest factor in how long probate takes is whether someone is actively pushing it forward. A passive attorney is often the reason estates drag on for years unresolved. See our approach to complex probate →

Yes. In Texas, a will can be contested on grounds including: lack of testamentary capacity (the decedent did not understand what they were signing), undue influence (someone pressured the decedent into signing), fraud, and improper execution.

Will contests are litigation — not paperwork. They require a litigator, not a form-filer. This firm handles contested will matters in the Rio Grande Valley and across Texas.

When someone dies without a will (intestate), Texas law determines who inherits based on the family structure. Surviving spouses, children, and other relatives inherit in a specific order defined by statute — but the application of these rules to complex family structures (blended families, estranged relatives, undocumented property) often requires a formal heirship determination proceeding.

We handle heirship determination proceedings to establish rightful heirs and prevent unauthorized claimants from seizing assets.

Breach of Contract

Yes — oral contracts are enforceable in Texas, with some exceptions. Certain agreements (real estate transactions, contracts lasting more than one year) must be in writing to be enforceable. But many business arrangements and personal agreements made verbally are valid contracts that can be litigated.

The challenge with verbal contracts is proof. Without a written record, the dispute becomes a credibility and evidence battle — which is exactly where a prosecutorial approach to building a case matters most. See how we approach contract disputes →

Texas allows recovery of actual damages (the economic loss caused by the breach), consequential damages (foreseeable downstream losses), and in some cases attorney's fees. Punitive damages are generally not available for pure contract claims.

Where a contract breach also involves fraud or misrepresentation, additional remedies — including exemplary damages — may be available under the Texas DTPA. Learn more about breach of contract →

Medical Professionals & Licensing

When the TMB opens an investigation, you will receive a written notice of complaint. You will be given an opportunity to respond — and that response is one of the most important documents in your case. An unrepresented response that is too detailed, too defensive, or inadvertently inconsistent can seriously damage your position.

The moment you receive a TMB complaint notice, you should contact an attorney before responding to anything. See our medical professional defense →

TMB investigations vary significantly in length — from a few months for straightforward matters to well over a year for complex cases. The timeline depends on the nature of the complaint, the responsiveness of the parties, and whether the matter proceeds to a formal hearing.

Having counsel who moves aggressively — preparing a parallel investigation and not simply reacting — can significantly affect both the timeline and the outcome. Learn about our approach to TMB defense →

Yes. A TMB complaint — even an unresolved one — may trigger reporting obligations to the National Practitioner Data Bank (NPDB), which hospitals and credentialing bodies routinely query. This can affect hospital privileges, credentialing with insurers, and employment opportunities.

Managing the NPDB implications of a TMB matter is part of how we approach medical professional defense. It is not just about the complaint — it is about protecting the entire career.

This firm has a dedicated practice area specifically for healthcare professionals — physicians, CNMs, and licensed providers facing TMB complaints, hospital privilege disputes, NPDB concerns, and healthcare employment matters. Enter the medical professional experience →
