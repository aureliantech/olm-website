What's Actually at Stake

Your license is not the only thing on the line.

A Texas Medical Board complaint threatens every system that depends on your license — your income, your hospital affiliations, your prescribing authority, and the career trajectory you've spent a decade or more building. The NPDB filing that follows any disciplinary action is permanent and visible to every credentialing body in the country.

For early-career physicians, the stakes are compounded. A complaint before age 40 doesn't just affect this year's income — it forecloses opportunities, faculty appointments, and leadership roles that have not yet materialized. The career earnings exposure runs into the millions.

For physicians in private practice, an active investigation can disrupt the patient relationships, referral networks, and staff stability that took years to build — before any ruling is made.

The Defense Strategy

The Counter Protocol: we build our record before they build theirs.

Most attorneys wait for the board to move. They receive the complaint, draft a brief acknowledgment, and begin preparing for the eventual hearing. By the time they're ready, the board has had months to build its record — unopposed.

The Counter Protocol activates the day you retain us. We launch an independent parallel investigation immediately — gathering exculpatory evidence, building the factual timeline, and constructing an evidence-anchored rebuttal designed to force a dismissal decision before a hearing is ever scheduled.

The initial written response to the board is the single most consequential document in your defense. A passive response is a missed opportunity that cannot be recovered. An evidence-anchored rebuttal forces the board to make a decision.

What Triggers a Complaint

The complaint does not have to be meritorious to be dangerous.

→
Standard of Care Allegations Patient outcome complaints — the most common complaint type, and the one most dependent on the quality of the initial written response.
→
Prescribing Practice Complaints Controlled substance prescribing patterns, opioid-related complaints, and DEA referrals to the TMB — frequently triggered by audit algorithms, not individual complaints.
→
Hospital-Initiated Complaints Complaints filed by or at the direction of employers, health systems, or credentialing committees — often coinciding with employment disputes or peer review actions.
→
Boundary & Conduct Complaints Allegations of unprofessional conduct, boundary violations, or inappropriate communication — often the hardest to disprove without an aggressive, evidence-first response strategy.
→
Documentation & Billing Irregularities Incomplete records, billing practice concerns, or upcoding allegations — where the documentary record itself becomes the defense.
→
Impairment Allegations Substance use, mental health, or fitness-to-practice concerns — where the complaint's premise is immediately damaging to reputation before any ruling is made.

The wrong defense posture is worse than no defense at all.

A passive attorney who files a brief acknowledgment and prepares for an eventual hearing has given the board months of unopposed record-building time. That time cannot be recovered. If any of the following describes your situation, a case transfer may be warranted.

Warning Signal

Your attorney has gone quiet. Weeks pass with no updates. You are not sure what, if anything, is being done on your case.

Warning Signal

Your attorney is recommending accepting an ISC resolution. You have not been told whether dismissal was still achievable — or what the NPDB filing will mean permanently.

Warning Signal

The initial written response filed on your behalf did not include counter-evidence. It acknowledged the complaint and requested time. That window may now be closed.
